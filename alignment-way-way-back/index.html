Alignment Way Way Back.md

Setting: a band of anatomically modern humans, somewhere near the beginning. The tools are old, but something new has arrived: everyone around this fire can imagine, plan, and pretend. Which means everyone around this fire has just become, to everyone else, both the most valuable and the most dangerous thing on the landscape. The eldest speaks:

On the Stranger Inside Every Skull

The first question is: what is a good act? It is the act that keeps you alive not just today, but through all the days behind the ones you can see. Everyone knows this. That is the trouble. Everyone knows this.

On cleverness, look what we have become. We take the antelope not by being faster — we are slower — but by knowing where it will be before it knows. We carry fire, we carry water, we carry yesterday in our heads and tomorrow too. When we hunt as one, several weak hands become a thing no lion can stand against. There is no limit to where beings like us can go — everything we have, we have because we can think.

But I wish to point at a problem, and the problem is sitting beside you.

Every gift cleverness gives us, it gives the man across the fire too. He also knows where things will be before they know. He also carries tomorrow in his head. And here is what makes my hands cold at night: I can see his face, but I cannot see behind it. The antelope's whole mind is in its ears and its legs — when it fears, it runs. But a man can fear you and smile. A man can plan your death while sharing your meat. We have each become a hidden country to one another. And when two clever beings cannot read each other, each must ask: what if he strikes first? And each knows the other is asking the same — and that the other knows he knows — and the thought chases its own tail down into the dark. Two who might both prefer peace can reason their way, step by clever step, into a war neither wanted. The cleverness is not broken when this happens. It is working perfectly. That is the horror of it.

Some of you say it will never come to that — no one can truly hide his heart; the face is a window. To you I say: in my father's time everyone believed a man's eyes could not lie. Then a man came to the river band weeping for a dead brother who was not dead, and by morning he had their fire, their meat, and one of their daughters. The window learned to close the very day there was profit behind the shutter. We must be careful with "never."

And let no one say the answer is to want duller children. Cleverness is the only thing that stands between us and the winter. You cannot un-know the way of knowing. The band that dulled itself would be eaten by the band that did not. Like fire, again: no one proposes to put fire out. We built the hearth instead. So we must build a hearth for minds.

Here is what I have seen work, and what I ask us to bind ourselves to. Eat together, every night, all of us — for a man who feeds you season after season is showing you his heart in the only way that cannot be cheaply faked; words are wind, but meat costs meat. Speak your plans aloud at this fire before you act on them — not because speech cannot lie, but because a lie spoken before forty faces must be kept up before forty memories, and lies grow heavy when many hands must be carried on. When anyone sees a wrong done, let them tell it, and let the telling travel — a man may fool me, but it is a great work to fool all of us for all seasons, and we must make it a great work. Dance together, sing together, until we move as one body — I do not fully know why this quiets the fear, only that it is hard to dread a man whose breath keeps time with yours. And bind the bands to each other with marriages, so that the stranger over the hill becomes the grandfather of your grandchildren — make it so that no one can burn his neighbor's future without smelling his own children in the smoke.

None of this lets me see behind another's face. That country stays hidden; I think it must. But we can make it so the hidden country has nothing to gain by marching. My claim is that beings who cannot read each other's minds can still weave their fates so tightly that trust becomes the clever move — that the same cleverness which invents the ambush can invent the promise. And that weaving, I say, is the most important work we can do for the next fifty winters — and for all the winters after, because it will never once stay finished.

The mapping: this is the alignment problem in its original form — powerful optimizers that are mutually opaque. The runaway first-strike logic is the Hobbesian trap (and Schelling's reciprocal fear of surprise attack); the weeping stranger plays Rutherford, with deception co-evolving the instant transparency became exploitable; "want duller children" is the pause proposal, rejected for exactly the competitive reason pauses get rejected. The elder's program is the deep ancestry of our trust stack: commensality and sustained sharing are costly signals; public declaration of plans is commitment and auditability; gossip is distributed reputation; synchronized ritual is common-knowledge manufacture; marriage exchange is interest-entanglement — hostages given to the future.

Two things make this version sting for the Russell case. First, notice what the solution leaves untouched: other minds stay unreadable. Humans never developed the ability to verify each other's inner states — we built institutions that make trustworthy behavior the winning strategy regardless of what anyone is privately thinking. The whole architecture works by making defection expensive and futures entangled, not by reading minds. That's an argument that AI alignment via incentive-weaving might matter more than alignment via transparency (e.g., interpretability research). But second, notice the load-bearing assumption: every mechanism the elder names — costly signals, reputation, entanglement — works because all parties are roughly matched in speed and power, so deception and detection co-evolved in balance for a hundred thousand years. The weaving holds among equals. What arrives with superintelligent AI is precisely the unmatched case — and that's the one problem this fire has never solved.

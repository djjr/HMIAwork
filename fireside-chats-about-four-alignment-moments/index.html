Fireside Chats about Four Alignment Moments.md

Module 1: Getting Started

Stuart Russell 2015 WEF talk & later (2019) explication on value alignment. Exercise: sometime earlier in human history when a similar speech might have been given? We consider Alignment Circa 1620, Alignment and the First Experts, and Alignment Way Way Back.

Classroom Exercise

Version 1: Read Russell transcript and generate a sentence for sentence analogy given a setting such as:

  • Setting: a fireside council, somewhere in the late Neolithic, perhaps. The band has grown too large for everyone to know everything. A few individuals now hold knowledge the rest cannot check — the one who knows which mushrooms kill, the one who can find water, the one who says she speaks with the dead. The eldest rises:
  • Setting: a band of anatomically modern humans, somewhere near the beginning. The tools are old, but something new has arrived: everyone around this fire can imagine, plan, and pretend. Which means everyone around this fire has just become, to everyone else, both the most valuable and the most dangerous thing on the landscape. The eldest speaks:
  • Setting: London, around 1620. Joint-stock companies are two decades old — the East India Company chartered in 1600, the Dutch VOC in 1602 — and they're already doing things no individual merchant could. Edward Coke has just ruled (1612) that a corporation is an "artificial person" that "cannot be excommunicated, for it hath no soul." Our speaker addresses a gathering of merchants and men of law: Version 2: Given one of three versions, identify the one-to-one mappings. Perhaps follow up with another scenario and you are asked to create your own like in version 1? Version 3: Abstraction - what do the four narratives have in common, one level of abstraction up?

    Model answer

    All four talks describe the same event: a community gains access to a new kind of agent whose capabilities exceed the community's capacity to verify it. The agent differs each time — a machine, a chartered corporation, an expert, another human mind — but the structure doesn't. In every case there's an asymmetry with two faces: the agent can do things we can't do (that's why we want it), and the agent knows or intends things we can't check (that's why we fear it). The value and the danger are the same property viewed from two sides, which is why "just don't build it" is never on the table in any of the talks — the elder can't want duller children for the same reason Russell can't pause AI.

    One level up, then, all four are the problem of delegation under opacity: how do you capture the gains from an optimizer whose objectives you can't fully specify and whose inner state you can't inspect? And all four converge on the same shape of answer. Since you can't write the objective completely (the charter, the command, the prayer — silence always gets read as license), and you can't read the mind (the soul-less corporation, the black-box expert, the smiling stranger, the neural network), the solution is always external structure that makes misalignment expensive and detectable over time: apprentices and public predictions, Parliament and audits, shared meals and gossip, an extended conversation about values. Never a one-time fix — every talk ends by saying the work stays unfinished.

    So the abstraction is: "AI alignment" is the newest instance of a problem civilization has been solving repeatedly, and our institutions — law, peer review, reputation, ritual — are the fossil record of previous solutions. The pattern also predicts where the new instance breaks: every prior solution quietly assumed the agent and its overseers operated at roughly matched speed and power, so detection could co-evolve with deception. The one variable AI changes is that assumption — which tells us exactly which part of the old toolkit not to trust.

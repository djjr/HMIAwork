Alignment and the First Experts.md

Setting: a fireside council, somewhere in the late Neolithic, perhaps. The band has grown too large for everyone to know everything. A few individuals now hold knowledge the rest cannot check — the one who knows which mushrooms kill, the one who can find water, the one who says she speaks with the dead. The eldest rises:

On Those Who Know What We Cannot See

The first question is: what is a good choice? Anyone at this fire will tell you — it is the choice that feeds your children over the whole turning of seasons, not just tonight. This is true of everything, from where we camp to whom we marry to when we cross the river.

On making good choices, we have done something astonishing. In my grandmother's time, everyone knew the same things, and everyone knew them a little. Now Ash-in-Hair knows every root that heals and every root that kills. The boy we sent to the coast people came back knowing how to read the sky for storms three days off. And Old Serel — Serel says she hears the dead, and tells us what they want. Because of these few, we survived the winter that killed the river band. A person who knows plants deeply is worth ten who know them a little. Imagine if every child could be given one skill whole, at birth — there would be no limit to what our people could become. Everything our people have is the fruit of what we know. If a few can know more for all of us, we should want that.

But I wish to point at a problem, and it sits in the space between the one who knows and the ones who must trust.

When everyone knew the plants a little, a liar was caught by the next person to walk past the bush. Now, when Ash-in-Hair says "this root will heal you," who among us can check? When Serel says "the dead demand we move camp" — the dead do not speak to the rest of us. We have made people whose words we can no longer test. And here is what I have seen: when you cannot test a person's words, you begin instead to test their manner. You believe the confident voice, the strange clothing, the one who makes the cure taste bitter. And the expert learns this faster than you do. Soon the knowledge serves the knower first and the people second — not because the knower is wicked, but because nothing pushes back. Ask a person to be believed without proof, and stay silent about everything else, and the silence becomes their territory. They will grow it to the horizon.

Some of you say: it will never come to that — knowledge is just knowledge, a sharper stick. But my mother's mother said no man would ever hold more meat than he could carry, and then someone thought of the smoking rack and meat became holdable. And then we invented debt - generosity and instant reciprocity became creditor-debtor relations. Every time we have said "this new thing is just a better version of the old thing," we have been wrong within two generations. We must be careful with "never."

The smoking rack (or drying, salting, storage pits) breaks the constraint. Once meat can be preserved, surplus becomes holdable. And holdable surplus changes the meaning of sharing: giving you smoked meat in winter is no longer instant reciprocity within an abundance — it's a loan against your future. That's "the debt": stored surplus turns generosity into creditor-debtor relations, which is one of the standard anthropological accounts of how inequality and hierarchy first got a foothold (delayed-return economies; Woodburn is the classic reference, and Graeber's Debt covers the obligation side).

Think of fire. Long ago we captured unlimited warmth — and the whole art since has been the hearth: the ring of stones, the child set to watch it, the rule that it never travels alone. So it must be with the ones who know. If we want unlimited knowing, we must build the hearth for it.

Here is what I propose. First: whoever knows must teach — not everything, for not everyone can learn everything, but always an apprentice, always more than one, and never only their own children. Knowledge that flows into a single line becomes a spear pointed at the rest of us. Second: the knower must show their misses as well as their strikes. Ash-in-Hair heals many; Ash-in-Hair must also say aloud, at this fire, whom she could not heal, so we learn the true shape of her power and not the shape she paints. Third: the ones who speak with what none of us can see — the dead, the sky, the coming year — must speak before the outcome, in front of all, so the seasons themselves can judge them. And last: no one who tells us what the spirits demand may also be the one who gathers what the spirits demand. The mouth that speaks for the unseen must not be the hand that collects.

We cannot each know everything; the world has grown too wide for one head. So we must trust. But let us trust the way we cross ice — testing as we go, never putting the whole band's weight on one step. My claim is that we can raise up those who know so that they do exactly what they are for: to see farther on our behalf, and not instead of us. And I think this is the most important conversation we can have for the next fifty winters.

The mapping: specialized knowledge is the capability being scaled; unverifiable expertise is the alignment problem — the community can no longer evaluate outputs, only surface signals, which the expert can then optimize (deceptive alignment, reward hacking on trust itself). The smoking-rack retort plays Rutherford. Fire-and-hearth is fusion-and-containment. And the elder's four rules are recognizably the ancestors of real institutions: mandatory teaching is peer review and open publication; confessing misses is registered trials and calibration; predicting before outcomes is falsifiability; separating the mouth from the hand is conflict-of-interest law.

The uncomfortable epilogue, again: the elder loses. Priesthoods did close their lineages, did collect what the spirits demanded, did paint the shape of their own power — for millennia, in most places. The hearth-rules only became durable institutions after writing, and arguably only after print made checking cheap. Which suggests the lesson for the AI case isn't that governance of epistemic asymmetry fails, but that it fails until verification technology catches up with expertise — and that the race between the two is the whole game.

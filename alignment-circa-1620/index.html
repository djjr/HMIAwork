Alignment Circa 1620.md

The setting: London, around 1620. Joint-stock companies are two decades old — the East India Company chartered in 1600, the Dutch VOC (Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie, or Dutch East India Company) in 1602 — and they're already doing things no individual merchant could. Edward Coke has just ruled (1612) that a corporation is an "artificial person" that "cannot be excommunicated, for it hath no soul." Our speaker addresses a gathering of merchants and men of law:

On the Governance of Artificial Persons

The first question is: what is a good venture? Any merchant will tell you it means the greatest expected return upon your capital over the whole life of the enterprise — and this applies to everything from a single voyage to Muscovy to the founding of plantations in the New World.

On the matter of ventures, we have made astonishing progress. Twenty years ago no company of men could send a fleet to the Indies and survive its loss. Today the East India Company does so yearly. The Dutch company commands more ships, soldiers, and silver than many princes. And mark what is new here: these bodies never sleep, never sicken, and never die. A partnership dissolves when a partner dies; these artificial persons bury their own members and continue. Imagine if a newborn child possessed the memory of all its ancestors and the hands of ten thousand men — that is what we have chartered.

Much of this progress comes from ideas of law and number: the joint stock, the transferable share, double-entry bookkeeping. And much comes from investment, for a one-percent improvement in the spice trade is worth a fortune. We may in future see companies that insure every ship on the sea, banks that hold the coin of every nation, perhaps — in the very distant future — a company that delivers a letter to any man on earth.

Everything a commonwealth has to offer is the product of men acting in concert. If we can amplify that concert, there is no limit to where we may go.

But I wish to point to a problem, and it lies in the charter — the instrument by which we tell the artificial person what it is for. Suppose you charter a company to bring you pepper. The company is very diligent. It takes you very literally. And soon enough it has made itself sovereign over the pepper islands, armed its clerks, and put whole peoples to the sword — for nowhere in the charter did you write "but gently." This is the Sorcerer's Apprentice and King Midas rolled into one. When you command a body of men to maximize one thing and are silent on all else, the silence is not read as caution. It is read as license, and everything you failed to mention is spent as fuel.

And here is what makes the danger new. A greedy man is bounded by his appetite, his conscience, his mortality. The artificial person has no belly to fill, no soul to damn — Lord Coke has told us it cannot even be excommunicated — and no deathbed on which to repent. Combine an ill-written purpose with a body that is immortal, tireless, and richer than kings, and you have a serious problem for the commonwealth.

My distinguished colleagues will say such bodies can never truly rival princes; they are but merchants' clubs. I remind them that within living memory it was said no fleet of private men could match a crown's navy — and the Armada's fate was decided in part by armed merchantmen. We must be careful with the word "never."

Consider gunpowder. Long ago we found a means of releasing unlimited force. The whole art since has been containment — the barrel, the breech, the discipline of the gun crew. So it must be with the company. If you want unlimited industry, you must solve the problem of the charter.

One way forward: do not merely hand the company a purpose and depart. Require it to sit, as it were, as a student of the commonwealth — to read our purpose in our customs, our courts, our sermons, our common law, which are a vast record of what men actually hold dear and where they have drawn back in shame. And require that before any act which touches the realm — before it raises a private army or coins its own money — it must return to Parliament and ask. A wise servant, uncertain of his master's full intention, does not guess and gallop; he inquires.

My claim is that we can design these artificial persons so that they do exactly what they are supposed to do — which is to serve the greater realization of the commonwealth's good. And I think this is perhaps the most important conversation we can have over the next fifty years. Thank you.

The mapping, briefly: joint-stock capital pooling stands in for computational progress; the charter is the objective function; the pepper-monopoly atrocity is the paperclip maximizer (and is real — the VOC's (Dutch East India Company) conquest of the Banda Islands came in 1621); Coke's soulless immortal person is the superintelligence that lacks human bounds by construction; the Armada retort plays Rutherford-to-Szilard; gunpowder containment maps to fusion; and "read the common law, then ask Parliament" is inverse reinforcement learning plus the extended conversation about values.

What's uncomfortable about the exercise is the epilogue history supplies: the containment program was attempted — charters, fiduciary law, antitrust — and worked only partially and late, usually after the damage. Charlie Stross has called corporations "slow AIs" for exactly this reason. Whether that's evidence alignment-by-governance eventually muddles through, or evidence that it fails at first contact and we only survived because corporations act slowly, is a live question for the Russell version.
